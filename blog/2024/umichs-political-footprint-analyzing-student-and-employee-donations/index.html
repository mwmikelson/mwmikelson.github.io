The Michigan Daily’s Web team looked at data on political donations from individuals who listed the University of Michigan as their employer in donations to political candidates between Jan. 1, 2007, and Oct. 15, 2024 to investigate one aspect of political engagement among the U-M Community. Over this period, there were more than 294,000 donations from U-M employees and students. The 2020 election cycle alone accounted for 39% of the donations made by U-M affiliates between 2007 and 2024. The peak amount donated by professors was in October 2020 at $306,945 one month before the election.

As of Oct. 15, donations for the 2024 cycle have not surpassed 2022. However, this is likely to change when campaigns submit filings with the Federal Election Commission (FEC) after the election concludes as historically most donations come in October.


Filtering by how the donors self-described their occupation, we grouped donations into students, professors and people associated with Michigan Medicine (i.e. nurses, doctors, medical staff). Professors very consistently donate more than both students and medical staff. However, the amount of money donated by students is likely underrepresented due to students with other jobs who do not select student as their occupation. While students earn less money than professors, they outnumber faculty 15 to 1.

Across U.S. Senate, House of Representatives and Presidential elections, the vast majority of individuals employed by the University donated to Democratic candidates. Democrats received 94% of donations from those employed by the University, while Republicans received 6%. Relative to these quantities, all other parties received nearly 0% of donations from University employees. This is consistent with national trends, as those with a college degree are more likely to support Democratic candidates.

48% of donations from those associated with the University went to candidates in Michigan, the highest percentage for a single state. Georgia received the next most donations, with 11% followed by Arizona and California. Of the donations to Arizona, 89% were associated with the 2020 Senate race between current senator Mark Kelly and former senator Martha McSally.

However, 75% of the donations to the state of Georgia were associated with the two Senate races in 2020. Those races decided control of the Senate leading to heightened national attention. Donations to the races from U-M employees drastically increased after November 2020 as the stakes of the runoffs in deciding control of the Senate became clear.

During the 2020 presidential primaries, U-M employees and students donated almost $660,000. Sen. Bernie Sanders, I-Vt., received over $38,000 more in donations than Vice President Joe Biden, although Sanders received fewer votes in the primaries.

If you want to find out where your professors donated, use OpenSecrets and search for their names.

How we classified the data:

We classified political parties, states, and political office mostly by hand. If the name of the political action committee (PAC) contained the name of a political party or state, it was automatically classified as that party or state. Independent is used for both candidates who aren’t running as part of a political party and for political action committees that are associated with professions. Some PACs are listed by OpenSecrets with a viewpoint as either conservative or liberal in which case they were classified as Republican or Democratic, respectively.
